Automating Catalyst Characterization: An AI-Powered Tool for TEM Particle Size Analysis

A walk through how I built a custom computer vision tool, starting with painstaking data annotation and ending with a working desktop app that makes TEM particle measurements fast and reliable.

Published on: August 19, 2025

The High Cost of a Simple Question

In materials science, a deceptively simple question shapes the outcome of an experiment: “What is the size distribution of my nanoparticles?” Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM) images hold that answer, but turning those images into meaningful numbers has long been a frustrating bottleneck.

Manually outlining hundreds of particles is not only slow, it also introduces inconsistency. Results vary depending on who’s doing the tracing, making the data less reliable. I built this tool to get around that barrier: replacing a time-consuming, error-prone process with one that is objective, automated, and dramatically faster.

The Development Journey: A Three-Step Process

Step 1: Teaching the AI with Professional Tools

An AI model is only as good as the data it learns from. To build a solid foundation, I turned to Roboflow, a platform for managing computer vision datasets. I uploaded 80 TEM images covering the range of our samples, from 5 nm to 50 nm scale bars, and drew polygon masks around several thousand particles. Roboflow’s annotation and versioning tools streamlined the process, and just as importantly, made sure the dataset was clean and reproducible.

Left: a raw TEM image. Right: the same image annotated in Roboflow, with each particle outlined by hand.

Step 2: Training a "Specialist" Model in the Cloud

Training a deep learning model takes a lot of computing power. To get access to GPUs without the price tag, I used Google Colab and the Detectron2 framework. Detectron2’s Mask R-CNN architecture is well-suited for instance segmentation, identifying and outlining individual objects in an image.

I fine-tuned the model on my annotated dataset until it could reliably pick out particles on images it had never seen before. The trained network was then saved into a single portable file, model_final.pth, which became the core “brain” of the application.

The trained model applied to a new TEM image: particles automatically identified and segmented.

Step 3: From a Model File to an Intuitive Desktop App

A model file on its own isn’t very practical for day-to-day lab work. The final step was building a simple desktop application in Python using Tkinter. This app acts as a wrapper around the AI model: it loads model_final.pth, processes the user’s TEM images, runs predictions, and turns the raw segmentation masks into useful outputs like particle diameters, summary statistics, and histograms. What used to be a command-line workflow is now point-and-click.

The Application in Action

In practice, the workflow is straightforward: select a folder of images, set the scale with two clicks, and press “Analyze.” Within seconds, the app generates side-by-side images for review, real-time stats like mean and standard deviation, and a particle size distribution histogram. If the AI mislabels something, it can be corrected with a click, and all results update instantly. Exporting the data is just as quick.

Challenges & Solutions

Like any AI model, this one has its limitations. The key was to acknowledge them and design the app so they didn’t get in the way of reliable results.

Challenge 1: False Positives

Occasionally the AI confuses the carbon support film or an imaging artifact for a particle. To catch these cases, I added a quick review step: users can click any incorrectly identified particle to remove it, with all plots and stats updating in real time.

Challenge 2: Overlapping Particles

Dense, overlapping clusters are the hardest cases. The model sometimes merges particles together or misses a few. While it performs very well on dispersed samples, I’m planning to add a manual “draw your own mask” feature so users can quickly add particles the AI overlooks. This will make the tool more flexible for challenging datasets.

Cracking the Code of CNT Growth

A deep dive into how transport phenomena govern deep-injection chemical vapor deposition, with an approachable summary and an optional technical breakdown.

AricKopke · August 20, 2025

When I began my Ph.D. research on carbon nanotube (CNT) synthesis, one question stood out: why is it still so hard to make high-quality CNTs at scale? After decades of research, the process often felt more like an art than a science.

The answer, it turns out, lies in the complex dance of gases inside the reactor. In conventional floating catalyst chemical vapor deposition (FCCVD), hot gases swirl and recirculate near the entrance, often deactivating the catalyst particles before they can even start growing nanotubes.

Deep injection (DI) flips the script. By injecting the catalyst and precursor gases through a long, cooled tube, we launch a stable, high-speed jet deep into the reactor's hot zone. This jet punches through the recirculation, creating a well-defined reaction zone where the cold jet meets the hot reactor environment. This interface is the sweet spot for CNT growth.

A Tale of Four Gases

To prove that the flow regime—not just the chemistry—was the key, we systematically swapped the primary carrier gas (Argon) with others. Here’s what we found:

  • Argon ↔ Nitrogen: Virtually no change. Their similar densities and thermal properties meant the flow structure remained intact.
  • Helium: Being much lighter, Helium shortened the jet. Below ~30% He, the system was fine. Past that, the jet no longer effectively penetrated the hot zone, and productivity plummeted.
  • Hydrogen: This was the most dramatic. It had the same transport effect as Helium (shortening the jet) but also introduced a chemical effect: at high concentrations, it actively suppressed the carbon species needed for growth, causing a total collapse.

The key takeaway: Growth is not random. It happens precisely where the cold, stable jet meets the hot, recirculating gases. Transport phenomena define this zone, and chemistry determines what happens inside it.

Deep Dive: Flow Maps, Dimensionless Numbers, and Why Jet Length Rules

The Transport Framework: Reynolds and Rayleigh Numbers

To quantify the balance between the jet and the surrounding flow, we used two dimensionless numbers: the Reynolds number ($Re$), representing the jet's inertial force, and the Rayleigh number ($Ra$), representing the buoyancy-driven natural convection. These were evaluated at the cannula exit using temperatures derived from our CFD simulations.

As we substituted Argon with lighter gases like Helium and Hydrogen, both $Re$ and $Ra$ decreased, signaling a weaker jet and less dominant buoyancy forces. This confirmed our hypothesis that changes in gas properties were directly altering the fundamental flow regime.

CFD Insights: Visualizing the Growth Zone

Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) simulations provided a clear picture of the reactor's interior. The maps revealed a cold, high-velocity jet down the centerline surrounded by a large, hot recirculation cell along the walls. The "sweet spot" for CNT growth is the shear layer between these two zones. With Helium, the jet visibly shortened, shrinking this productive interface. With Hydrogen, the flow map was similar to Helium, but chemical simulations showed a quenching of key growth precursors (like methyl radicals) in the reaction zone.

Jet Length as the Critical Parameter

Ultimately, productivity tracked one key parameter: mixed jet length. Our results showed a clear threshold. Once the injection Reynolds number ($Re$) fell below ≈260 (as it did with high Helium fractions), the jet length collapsed, and conversion fell sharply. This demonstrates that for a given chemistry, maintaining a sufficiently long jet is the primary requirement for high-yield synthesis in a DI-FCCVD reactor.

Takeaways

  • Transport is paramount: The physical properties of the carrier gas (density, viscosity, thermal conductivity) are not passive variables; they actively shape the flow field that enables or disables growth.
  • Flow and chemistry must align: An ideal flow regime is necessary but not sufficient. If the gas chemistry actively suppresses growth precursors (as with high H₂ concentrations), even a perfect flow structure will fail.
