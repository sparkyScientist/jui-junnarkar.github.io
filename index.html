Automating Catalyst Characterization: An AI-Powered Tool for TEM Particle Size Analysis

A deep dive into the development of a custom computer vision tool, from painstaking data annotation to a fully-functional desktop application.

Published on: August 19, 2025

The High Cost of a Simple Question

In materials science, a fundamental question dictates the success of an experiment: "What is the size distribution of my nanoparticles?" The answer, found in Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM) images, is critical. Yet, the process of getting that answer is a well-known bottleneck that slows down innovation.

Manually outlining hundreds of particles is not just slow; it's a source of significant experimental error. The results are subjective, varying from person to person, introducing inconsistency that can compromise research findings. I built this tool to eliminate this bottleneck, replacing a manual, error-prone process with an objective, automated, and incredibly fast solution.

The Development Journey: A Three-Step Process

Step 1: Teaching the AI with Professional Tools

An AI model is only as smart as the data it's trained on. To manage this process efficiently, I used Roboflow, a platform designed for computer vision dataset management. I uploaded 80 TEM images representing the full diversity of our samplesâ€”with magnifications ranging from 5nm to 50nm scale barsâ€”and used Roboflow's annotation tools to draw a precise polygon mask around several thousand individual catalyst particles. This not only streamlined the labeling process but also helped in organizing and versioning the dataset, which is crucial for creating a reproducible and robust model.

Left: A raw TEM image. Right: The same image after manual annotation in Roboflow, with each particle's mask clearly defined.

Step 2: Training a "Specialist" Model in the Cloud

Training a deep learning model requires immense computational power. I leveraged Google Colab for free access to high-performance GPUs, which are essential for this task. Using the Detectron2 framework, I configured a Mask R-CNN modelâ€”an architecture perfect for instance segmentation (detecting and outlining individual objects).

I fed my annotated dataset to this model and began the fine-tuning process. This technique takes a powerful model pre-trained on millions of general images and re-trains it to become a "specialist" on one specific task. After many iterations of training and validation, I had a highly accurate model that could identify catalyst particles on new, unseen images. The entire "brain" of this trained model was then exported into a single, portable file: `model_final.pth`.

The result of the training: the AI model automatically identifying and segmenting particles on a previously unseen image.

Step 3: From a Model File to an Intuitive Desktop App

A model file on its own isn't a useful tool. The final step was to build a user-friendly interface that anyone in the lab could use. I developed a standalone desktop application using Python and the Tkinter library. This application serves as an intuitive "wrapper" around the powerful AI model. When a user runs an analysis, the app loads the `model_final.pth` file, processes the user's images, feeds them to the AI for prediction, and then translates the raw output (pixel masks) into meaningful data: particle diameters, statistics, and a final histogram plot. This turned a complex command-line process into a simple, point-and-click solution.

The Application in Action

The final product is a seamless, end-to-end workflow. The user selects a folder of images, calibrates the scale with two clicks, and hits "Analyze." In seconds, the results appear: a side-by-side view for verification, real-time statistics (mean, std dev), and a combined particle size distribution (PSD) histogram. Any errors can be corrected with a simple click, and all data can be exported with one more.

Challenges & Solutions

No AI is perfect. Acknowledging limitations and building solutions for them is a critical part of creating a trustworthy scientific tool.

Challenge 1: False Positives

Occasionally, the AI might incorrectly identify a piece of the carbon support or an artifact as a particle. To ensure 100% data integrity, I implemented the "human-in-the-loop" correction feature. In the app's review screen, the user can simply click on any incorrectly identified particle, and it is instantly removed from the dataset, with all statistics and plots updating in real-time.

Challenge 2: Overlapping Particles (False Negatives)

The model's biggest challenge is discerning individual particles in highly dense, overlapping clusters, where it sometimes misses a few. While the current version excels on dispersed samples, the next feature I plan to add is a manual annotation tool. This will allow a user to draw a new mask directly onto an image inside the app to include any particles the AI may have missed, providing a complete solution for even the most challenging images.

Ph.D. Research

Optimizing Carbon Nanotube Synthesis via Multiphysics Modeling

Developing computational models to enhance Floating Catalyst Chemical Vapor Deposition (FCCVD).

My doctoral research focuses on optimizing the synthesis of Carbon Nanotubes (CNTs) in FCCVD reactors. By developing multi-physics computational models in OpenFOAM and COMSOL, I simulate the complex interplay of fluid dynamics, heat transfer, mass transfer, and aerosol dynamics within the reactor.

These model-guided insights allow for targeted experimental design and process optimization. A key achievement has been engineering a custom Deep Injection FCCVD setup, which, validated by my computational predictions, led to a 30% increase in carbon conversion during material synthesis.

This work combines fundamental science with practical engineering to create more efficient and scalable methods for producing advanced materials.
